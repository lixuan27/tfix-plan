01 — the question

What happens when an image model edits a video, frame by frame?

sourceper-frame edit

Two things happen at once.

It gets right

Content and structure. Every car, sign, pole and wire stays exactly where it was. The instruction lands on every frame.

It leaves behind

Flicker. Each frame is drawn on its own, so the appearance keeps changing under your eyes.

The whole project follows from this split: hand the content to an image model, and give the video model a single, small job.

02 — the task

So we split the problem in two, and learn only the second half.

One model is frozen. One model is trained. Everything the fixer needs to know arrives with the draft.

03 — why the split pays

How much is left to learn once the picture is already drawn?

Almost everything, once the image model commits, is already decided.

What remains is a small, structured, frame-to-frame correction — small enough to learn from clean video alone.

04 — the field

Where does everyone else spend their budget?

Pick a route.

They pay in data, and again in drift.

Edited videos have to be synthesized by a bigger model and filtered by a VLM — and weak pairs actively hurt the fine-tune. Editing then lives inside the recurrent state, so it drifts over minutes.

Measured in our harness: the leading open streaming editor reaches motion smoothness 0.993 while its dynamic degree sits at 0.25 — much of that smoothness comes from a scene that barely moves.

They pay in causality.

These methods look at keyframes or the whole clip, so they belong offline. Streaming asks for a model that decides frame t knowing only the past.

They pay with the edit itself.

Flow smoothing removes 43% of the flicker, blurs the frame, and takes the edit with it (edit preservation 0.80 → 0.75). An average treats an intended edit and an artifact as the same thing.

05 — what we hit ourselves

Two walls taught us where the real constraint lives.

Wall 1

Every fixer-side idea lands on the same curve.

Five interventions, one frontier. The “10× capacity” gain turned out to be reproducible by a zero-training pixel blend of the small model.

Wall 2

Give the model a shortcut, and it will take it.

Copying the clean source is the cheapest way to look temporally consistent. So we withhold the source and anchor the output to the draft instead.

07 — insight II

The two stages have to be tuned to each other.

This is the project’s real contribution: a strategy, not a loss term. Diagnose the frozen stage first; calibrate the learned stage against what the frozen one truly does.

08 — the system

The full pipeline. Click a stage.

Stage A — the editor frozen

Qwen-Image-Edit, 4-step distilled, run per frame. It keeps every car, sign and pole in place — the precondition for everything downstream. Its seed policy is the dial that matters.

2.2 s/frame at 720p, against 37 s/frame for the 40-step model, at almost the same sharpness.

Shared latent space frozen

A video VAE and a text encoder. We measured the codec’s own floor first: its round-trip flicker is 1.08 against a natural-video floor of 1.05, so the draft’s ~9 units of flicker are genuinely ours to remove.

Stage B — the fixer learned

A causal video transformer, started from a distilled streaming generator. The draft and the source enter through new input channels that begin at zero — training therefore starts exactly at the pretrained model and moves forward from there.

1.3B parameters. A 10× larger version matched it, so capacity stays where it is.

Streaming runtime

Chunk by chunk, with a fixed-size rolling cache whose positions are re-indexed to stay inside the trained range. Any stream length, constant memory, constant latency.

Output judged by eye first

Every checkpoint is watched before it is scored. Blur, frozen motion and a reverted edit all look excellent to a flicker metric — so the video decides, and the table confirms.

09 — the algorithm, in one picture

If the answer is already close, how far must the model travel?

We start the model at the draft. The remaining distance is short enough for a single step.

Anchored to the draft

A penalty holds the output at the draft’s appearance: time is free to change, looks stay put.

Trained without a clean source in view

The fixer works from the draft’s own appearance, so it learns to repair the edit rather than replace it.

10 — the training data

Where do training pairs come from, if edited video is off the table?

The reconstruction produces the same kind of flicker as a real edit — same temporal spectrum, same colour balance — at about a third of the amount. So we raise the amount with jitter that leaves appearance untouched, and the clean video stays the right answer.

The trap

A stronger prompt would reach the same amplitude by repainting the scene — and then the clean video answers a different question. Amplitude is a knob we turn while appearance holds still.

11 — evaluation

Three ways to look like a fix. How do we tell the real one?

Fake 1

Blur the frame

soft pixels flicker less

Fake 2

Freeze the scene

a still video is perfectly consistent

Fake 3

Undo the edit

the original never flickered

New metric

Source-anchored structure

Flow read from the source, features that ignore style, floor set by the source against itself. It sees through a blurred output.

New measurement

Flicker character

How much flicker, and what kind. It turns “does my training noise match the test noise?” into a number.

Protocol

Motion beside smoothness

Smoothness always travels with motion. A smooth, motionless result loses on the spot.

Hard gate

Edit preservation

Reported on every run — and the rule that caught our own failure: watch the video, then read the table.

These instruments retired two of our own claims. That is the job we built them for.

12 — the plan

Five stages. Each one carries a number it must hit. Click one.

Stage 0 · calibrate the frozen editor

Probe it with no edit at all. Sweep seed policy, steps and guidance. Measure both the amount and the kind of flicker. Confirm structure by eye.

GATE — PASSED

Draft flicker at or below 10 · reached 9.40, down from 48.4. Editor runs at 2.2 s/frame, structure exact.

FALLBACK IN HAND

Couple the noise to optical flow so it follows the content.

Stage 1 · measure the train–test gap

Build identity, mild and drastic drafts on the same clips, and compare their flicker spectra. Decide what the fixer should be trained on — the first question our mentor asked.

GATE — PASSED

Same kind of flicker confirmed (33% vs 34% high-frequency share). Amplitude gap measured: 2.07 vs 7.37.

FALLBACK IN HAND

Blend in real mild-edit drafts with a consensus target.

Stage 2 · train the fixer running

Three arms on real reconstruction pairs, each trained with the source withheld: a balanced anchor, a stronger appearance anchor, and a more faithful variant. Checkpoints every 500 steps; saturation near 1000.

GATE — BOTH, TOGETHER

Flicker down ≥30% on real edit drafts and edit preservation ≥0.93, judged on watched video: a night→day edit still reads as daytime.

FALLBACK IN HAND

Move the target to a styled video, so preserving the edit becomes the only way to satisfy the loss.

Stage 3 · driving domain, then the long horizon

Rebuild the reconstruction corpus on driving footage, retrain the winning arm, attach the rolling cache, and trace the drift curve across minute-long streams.

GATE

Metrics hold on driving clips · drift slope under a third of an end-to-end editor’s · latency flat with length.

WHY IT SHOULD HOLD

Flicker grows with organic texture; driving scenes are rigid, which favours us.

Stage 4 · the head-to-head

Two editing benchmarks plus driving footage. The competitor’s checkpoint runs in our harness, on our videos. Smoothness is always reported with motion beside it.

GATE

Temporal metrics beside a dedicated edit-supervised editor while keeping more motion — at zero paired data, one step, and free editor swaps.

THE BOUNDARY WE STATE

Per-frame sharpness follows the editor’s re-render. We raise it at the front end, or we say so plainly.

A stage opens when the one before it passes. An arm that blurs, freezes or reverts is retired at its first checkpoint.
